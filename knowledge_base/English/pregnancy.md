# Pregnancy

## Overview

Pregnancy is the period during which a baby develops inside a woman's uterus (womb). A normal pregnancy lasts approximately **40 weeks**, counted from the first day of the woman's last menstrual period (LMP). Pregnancy is divided into three stages known as trimesters, each involving important changes in both the mother and the developing baby.

Most pregnancies progress normally with regular antenatal care, proper nutrition, adequate rest, and healthy lifestyle choices. However, some pregnancies become high-risk and require closer monitoring by healthcare professionals.

Early antenatal care greatly improves the health and safety of both the mother and the baby.

---

# How Pregnancy Occurs

Pregnancy begins when:

1. An ovary releases an egg (ovulation).
2. A sperm fertilizes the egg.
3. The fertilized egg travels through the fallopian tube.
4. The embryo implants into the lining of the uterus.
5. The placenta develops to nourish the growing baby.

The placenta supplies oxygen and nutrients to the baby while removing waste products throughout pregnancy.

---

# Duration of Pregnancy

A full-term pregnancy usually lasts:

- Approximately **40 weeks**
- Around **9 months**
- Divided into **3 trimesters**

### First Trimester
Weeks 1–12

### Second Trimester
Weeks 13–27

### Third Trimester
Weeks 28–40

---

# The First Trimester (Weeks 1–12)

During this period:

- Major organs begin forming.
- The baby's heart starts beating.
- The brain develops rapidly.
- Arms and legs begin forming.
- The placenta develops.

Common maternal symptoms include:

- Missed menstrual period
- Morning sickness
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Breast tenderness
- Fatigue
- Frequent urination
- Mood changes
- Increased sense of smell

Miscarriage is most common during the first trimester, making early antenatal care especially important.

---

# The Second Trimester (Weeks 13–27)

Many women feel healthier during this stage.

The baby develops:

- Bones
- Muscles
- Facial features
- Hearing
- Fingerprints
- Hair

The mother may notice:

- Baby movements ("quickening")
- Increased appetite
- Growing abdomen
- Back pain
- Mild swelling of the feet
- Skin changes
- Stretch marks

Routine antenatal visits remain essential.

---

# The Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40)

The baby grows rapidly and prepares for birth.

Changes include:

- Increased weight gain
- Stronger fetal movements
- Braxton Hicks contractions
- Shortness of breath
- Frequent urination
- Difficulty sleeping
- Pelvic pressure

Towards the end of pregnancy, the baby's head usually moves down into the pelvis in preparation for labour.

---

# Common Signs of Pregnancy

Common early signs include:

- Missed menstrual period
- Positive pregnancy test
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Fatigue
- Breast enlargement
- Breast tenderness
- Darkening of the nipples
- Frequent urination
- Mood swings
- Increased appetite
- Food cravings
- Food aversions

These symptoms vary from one woman to another.

---

# Normal Body Changes During Pregnancy

Pregnancy causes many normal physical changes.

These include:

- Enlargement of the uterus
- Increased blood volume
- Weight gain
- Faster heartbeat
- Breast enlargement
- Darkening of the skin around the nipples
- Stretch marks
- Mild ankle swelling
- Increased vaginal discharge
- Changes in posture

Most of these changes are normal and improve after delivery.

# Nutrition During Pregnancy

Proper nutrition supports healthy fetal growth and maternal health.

Pregnant women should eat a balanced diet containing:

- Whole grains
- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Beans
- Eggs
- Fish
- Lean meat
- Milk
- Yogurt
- Healthy fats
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Key nutrients include:

## Folic Acid

Folic acid helps prevent neural tube defects.

Good sources include:

- Green leafy vegetables
- Beans
- Fortified cereals

Folic acid supplements are commonly recommended before conception and during early pregnancy.

---

## Iron

Iron supports the production of healthy red blood cells and helps prevent anemia.

Sources include:

- Lean meat
- Liver (consume only if advised by a healthcare professional due to high vitamin A content)
- Beans
- Spinach
- Fortified cereals

Iron supplements may be prescribed during pregnancy.

---

## Calcium

Calcium supports healthy bones and teeth.

Sources include:

- Milk
- Cheese
- Yogurt
- Sardines
- Green leafy vegetables

---

## Protein

Protein supports the growth of the baby.

Good sources include:

- Eggs
- Fish
- Chicken
- Lean beef
- Beans
- Soy products
- Groundnuts

---

## Vitamin C

Vitamin C improves iron absorption and supports immunity.

Sources include:

- Oranges
- Guava
- Mangoes
- Tomatoes
- Bell peppers
- Citrus fruits

---

## Water

Pregnant women should drink adequate clean water daily to prevent dehydration and support healthy circulation.

---

# Healthy Eating During Pregnancy

Eating a balanced diet helps both the mother and the growing baby remain healthy.

Pregnant women are encouraged to eat a variety of foods from all major food groups.

Recommended foods include:

- Whole grains
- Brown rice
- Maize
- Millet
- Oats
- Fresh fruits
- Green leafy vegetables
- Beans
- Peas
- Lentils
- Eggs
- Fish
- Chicken
- Lean beef
- Low-fat dairy products
- Groundnuts
- Healthy vegetable oils

Eating small, frequent meals may help reduce nausea and heartburn.

---

# Foods to Limit or Avoid

Certain foods may increase the risk of illness during pregnancy.

Pregnant women should avoid or limit:

- Alcohol
- Tobacco products
- Recreational drugs
- Unpasteurized milk
- Unpasteurized cheese
- Raw eggs
- Undercooked meat
- Raw fish
- Raw shellfish
- Excess caffeine
- Expired foods
- Foods prepared under poor hygienic conditions

Food safety is important throughout pregnancy.

Always wash fruits and vegetables before eating.

---

# Physical Activity During Pregnancy

Regular physical activity provides many health benefits during pregnancy.

Benefits include:

- Better blood circulation
- Reduced back pain
- Better sleep
- Improved mood
- Healthy weight gain
- Reduced constipation
- Better preparation for labour

Suitable activities include:

- Walking
- Swimming
- Prenatal stretching
- Light aerobic exercise
- Pregnancy yoga (under supervision)

Avoid:

- Contact sports
- Heavy lifting
- Activities with a high risk of falling
- Scuba diving
- Extreme heat exposure

Women with high-risk pregnancies should seek medical advice before exercising.

---

# Weight Gain During Pregnancy

Healthy weight gain supports the baby's growth.

The amount of recommended weight gain varies depending on the mother's weight before pregnancy.

Rapid or excessive weight gain should be discussed with a healthcare professional because it may indicate complications such as pre-eclampsia.

---

# Antenatal Care (ANC)

Antenatal care is the routine healthcare provided during pregnancy.

Women should begin antenatal care as early as possible, preferably during the first trimester.

Regular antenatal visits help:

- Monitor the baby's growth
- Detect pregnancy complications early
- Monitor the mother's health
- Provide nutritional advice
- Administer recommended vaccinations
- Prepare the mother for labour and delivery

Do not skip scheduled antenatal appointments.

---

# Routine Antenatal Tests

Healthcare providers may recommend:

- Blood pressure measurement
- Weight monitoring
- Urine testing
- Blood group testing
- Full blood count
- Blood sugar testing
- HIV screening
- Hepatitis B screening
- Syphilis screening
- Ultrasound scan
- Malaria testing where appropriate

Additional tests may be recommended depending on individual health needs.

---

# Recommended Vaccinations

Vaccination during pregnancy helps protect both the mother and the baby.

Healthcare providers may recommend:

- Tetanus-containing vaccine
- Influenza vaccine
- COVID-19 vaccine (where appropriate and according to local guidelines)

Always receive vaccines recommended by qualified healthcare professionals.

---

# Medicines During Pregnancy

Pregnant women should only take medicines prescribed or approved by qualified healthcare professionals.

Some medicines are safe during pregnancy, while others may harm the developing baby.

Never:

- Self-medicate
- Use antibiotics without prescription
- Use herbal medicines without professional advice
- Share medicines with others

Always inform healthcare providers that you are pregnant before receiving treatment.

---

# Mental Health During Pregnancy

Pregnancy can affect emotional wellbeing.

Many women experience:

- Mood swings
- Anxiety
- Fear of childbirth
- Stress
- Emotional sensitivity

Seek support from:

- Family members
- Healthcare providers
- Trusted friends
- Mental health professionals when necessary

Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or thoughts of self-harm require immediate professional help.

---

These include:

[O- Morning sickness
- Heartburn
- Constipation
- Back pain
- Leg cramps
- Swollen feet
- Fatigue
- Difficulty sleeping
- Frequent urination
- Mild shortness of breath

# High-Risk Pregnancy

Some pregnancies require closer medical supervision.

Risk factors include:

- Age below 18 years
- Age above 35 years
- Multiple pregnancy (twins or more)
- High blood pressure
- Diabetes
- Kidney disease
- Heart disease
- HIV infection
- Previous pregnancy complications
- Previous caesarean section
- Severe anemia
- Obesity

Women with high-risk pregnancies should attend all antenatal appointments and follow medical advice closely.

---

# Pregnancy Complications

Possible complications include:

- Miscarriage
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Gestational diabetes
- Pregnancy-induced hypertension
- Pre-eclampsia
- Eclampsia
- Placenta previa
- Placental abruption
- Premature labour
- Premature rupture of membranes
- Severe anemia
- Infections

Early diagnosis greatly improves outcomes for both mother and baby.

---

# Danger Signs During Pregnancy

Some symptoms during pregnancy require immediate medical attention.

Seek urgent medical care if you experience:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding
- Severe abdominal pain
- Severe headache that does not improve
- Blurred vision or loss of vision
- Swelling of the face or hands
- Convulsions or seizures
- Difficulty breathing
- Persistent high fever
- Persistent vomiting with inability to keep fluids down
- Leakage of fluid before labour begins
- Reduced or absent fetal movements after 28 weeks
- Severe chest pain
- Loss of consciousness

These symptoms may indicate serious pregnancy complications that require emergency treatment.

---

# Preparing for Labour

As the expected delivery date approaches, families should prepare for childbirth.

Preparation includes:

- Choosing a healthcare facility for delivery
- Identifying a skilled birth attendant
- Arranging transportation
- Saving emergency funds
- Packing a hospital bag
- Identifying a compatible blood donor if necessary
- Keeping emergency contact numbers available

Birth planning helps reduce delays during emergencies.

---

# Signs of Labour

Labour may begin naturally when the baby is ready for birth.

Common signs include:

- Regular painful contractions
- Lower back pain
- Rupture of membranes ("water breaking")
- Bloody mucus discharge (show)
- Increasing pelvic pressure

Women should go to their healthcare facility when labour begins or as advised by their healthcare provider.

---

# Stages of Labour

## First Stage

The cervix gradually opens (dilates) to allow the baby to pass.

This stage includes:

- Regular contractions
- Cervical dilation
- Progressive labour

---

## Second Stage

The baby is delivered through the birth canal.

The mother actively pushes during contractions under the guidance of healthcare professionals.

---

## Third Stage

After the baby is born, the placenta is delivered.

Healthcare providers monitor the mother closely to reduce the risk of excessive bleeding.

---

# Delivery Options

Depending on the mother's condition, delivery may occur through:

## Vaginal Delivery

This is the natural method of childbirth and is appropriate for many pregnancies.

---

## Caesarean Section (C-Section)

A Caesarean section is a surgical procedure used when vaginal delivery is not safe for the mother or baby.

Reasons may include:

- Fetal distress
- Breech presentation
- Placenta previa
- Previous Caesarean section (in selected cases)
- Obstructed labour
- Certain maternal medical conditions

The choice of delivery method should always be made by qualified healthcare professionals.

---

# After Delivery

The postpartum period begins immediately after childbirth and usually lasts about six weeks.

During this time, mothers should:

- Attend postnatal clinic visits
- Get adequate rest
- Eat nutritious meals
- Drink enough clean water
- Practice good personal hygiene
- Monitor for excessive bleeding
- Take prescribed medications
- Seek help if feeling emotionally overwhelmed

---

# Breastfeeding

Breastfeeding provides the best nutrition for most babies.

Exclusive breastfeeding is generally recommended for the first six months of life unless medically contraindicated.

Benefits include:

For the baby:

- Complete nutrition
- Stronger immunity
- Lower risk of infections
- Healthy growth and development

For the mother:

- Faster recovery after childbirth
- Reduced postpartum bleeding
- Strengthened mother–baby bonding
- Lower risk of certain diseases later in life

Breastfeeding support should be sought if difficulties occur.

---

# Family Planning After Pregnancy

Women should discuss family planning options with healthcare providers after delivery.

Available methods may include:

- Condoms
- Oral contraceptive pills
- Injectable contraceptives
- Implants
- Intrauterine devices (IUDs)
- Lactational Amenorrhea Method (LAM) for eligible breastfeeding mothers

Family planning helps improve maternal and child health by allowing adequate spacing between pregnancies.

---

# Emotional Well-being After Childbirth

Many women experience emotional changes after delivery.

These may include:

- Mild mood changes ("baby blues")
- Fatigue
- Anxiety
- Difficulty sleeping

If sadness, hopelessness, loss of interest, or thoughts of self-harm persist for more than two weeks, seek medical attention promptly, as these may be signs of postpartum depression.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## How soon should antenatal care begin?

A woman should begin antenatal care as soon as pregnancy is confirmed, preferably during the first trimester.

---

## Is exercise safe during pregnancy?

Yes.

Most healthy pregnant women benefit from regular moderate exercise unless advised otherwise by a healthcare professional.

---

## Can I travel during pregnancy?

Many women can travel safely during uncomplicated pregnancies. However, long journeys and travel late in pregnancy should be discussed with a healthcare provider.

---

## Is bleeding during pregnancy normal?

No.

Any vaginal bleeding during pregnancy should be assessed by a healthcare professional immediately.

---

## What should I do if the baby stops moving?

Reduced or absent fetal movement after about 28 weeks should be treated as an emergency. Go to the nearest healthcare facility immediately.

---

## Can pregnant women receive vaccinations?

Yes.

Certain vaccines are recommended during pregnancy to protect both the mother and baby. Always follow your healthcare provider's advice.

---

# Key Points

- Pregnancy normally lasts about 40 weeks.
- Early antenatal care improves the health of both mother and baby.
- Eat a balanced diet rich in essential nutrients.
- Avoid alcohol, smoking, recreational drugs, and unsafe medications.
- Attend all scheduled antenatal visits.
- Learn the danger signs of pregnancy.
- Prepare for labour before the expected delivery date.
- Deliver in a healthcare facility with skilled birth attendants whenever possible.
- Seek emergency medical care if warning signs develop.
