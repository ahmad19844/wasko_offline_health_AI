# Tuberculosis (TB)

## Overview

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. The disease most commonly affects the lungs (pulmonary TB), but it can also affect almost any part of the body, including the brain, kidneys, spine, lymph nodes, bones, and abdomen.

TB spreads through the air when a person with active pulmonary tuberculosis coughs, sneezes, laughs, sings, or talks. People nearby may inhale the bacteria and become infected.

Tuberculosis remains one of the leading infectious diseases worldwide. Fortunately, TB is both preventable and curable with early diagnosis and proper treatment.

---

# Causes

Tuberculosis is caused by the bacterium **Mycobacterium tuberculosis**.

The infection spreads through tiny airborne droplets released when an infected person:

- Coughs
- Sneezes
- Talks
- Laughs
- Sings

Another person may become infected after breathing in these droplets.

TB is **NOT** spread through:

- Handshakes
- Hugging
- Sharing food
- Sharing utensils
- Touching surfaces
- Mosquito bites
- Sharing clothes

---

# Risk Factors

People at higher risk include:

- Close family members of TB patients
- Healthcare workers
- People living with HIV
- Individuals with diabetes
- Smokers
- People who abuse alcohol
- Malnourished individuals
- Elderly adults
- Young children
- Prison inmates
- People living in overcrowded environments
- Individuals with weakened immune systems

---

# Types of Tuberculosis

## Pulmonary Tuberculosis

This is the most common type.

It affects the lungs and is usually contagious.

---

## Extrapulmonary Tuberculosis

TB may affect organs outside the lungs, including:

- Brain
- Spine
- Bones
- Kidneys
- Lymph nodes
- Abdomen
- Skin

Symptoms depend on the affected organ.

---

## Latent Tuberculosis Infection (LTBI)

In latent TB:

- The bacteria remain inactive.
- The person has no symptoms.
- The person cannot spread TB.
- Infection may become active later if immunity becomes weak.

---

# Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms often develop slowly over several weeks.

Common symptoms include:

- Persistent cough lasting more than two weeks
- Chest pain
- Cough producing sputum
- Coughing up blood
- Fever
- Night sweats
- Weight loss
- Loss of appetite
- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Difficulty breathing

Children and older adults may present differently.

---

# Severe Symptoms

Seek immediate medical attention if the patient develops:

- Coughing large amounts of blood
- Severe chest pain
- Severe difficulty breathing
- High fever
- Confusion
- Extreme weakness
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures
- Severe dehydration

These symptoms may indicate serious complications.

---

# Possible Complications

Without treatment, tuberculosis may cause:

- Permanent lung damage
- Respiratory failure
- Massive lung bleeding
- TB meningitis
- Kidney damage
- Bone destruction
- Heart involvement
- Spread throughout the body (miliary TB)
- Death

Early treatment significantly reduces complications.

---

# Diagnosis

Healthcare professionals may diagnose tuberculosis using:

- Medical history
- Physical examination
- Chest X-ray
- Sputum smear microscopy
- GeneXpert MTB/RIF test
- Sputum culture
- Tuberculin Skin Test (TST)
- Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA)
- CT scan (when necessary)

GeneXpert is commonly used because it rapidly detects TB bacteria and can identify rifampicin resistance.

---

# Treatment

Tuberculosis is curable.

Treatment involves taking a combination of anti-TB medicines for at least six months under medical supervision.

Common medicines include:

- Isoniazid
- Rifampicin
- Pyrazinamide
- Ethambutol

Patients should:

- Take medicines every day.
- Complete the full treatment course.
- Never stop treatment early.
- Attend all follow-up appointments.

Stopping treatment too early can lead to drug-resistant tuberculosis.

---

# Drug-Resistant Tuberculosis

Some TB bacteria become resistant to standard medicines.

Examples include:

- Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)
- Extensively drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB)

These forms require specialized treatment and closer medical supervision.

---

# Home Care

Patients should:

- Take medications exactly as prescribed.
- Eat nutritious meals.
- Drink adequate fluids.
- Rest sufficiently.
- Avoid smoking.
- Avoid alcohol.
- Cover the mouth when coughing.
- Keep living areas well ventilated.
- Attend regular clinic visits.

Patients should continue treatment even if they begin to feel better.

---

# Prevention

Tuberculosis can be prevented by:

- Early diagnosis
- Completing prescribed treatment
- Covering the mouth while coughing
- Good ventilation
- Avoiding overcrowded spaces where possible
- Screening close contacts
- BCG vaccination for infants where recommended
- Good nutrition
- HIV prevention and treatment
- Avoiding smoking

Community awareness plays an important role in reducing TB transmission.

---

# Nutrition During Recovery

A healthy diet supports recovery.

Recommended foods include:

- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Whole grains
- Beans
- Fish
- Lean meat
- Eggs
- Milk and dairy products
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Good nutrition helps strengthen the immune system.

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the nearest hospital immediately if the patient develops:

- Severe difficulty breathing
- Massive coughing of blood
- Persistent chest pain
- Confusion
- High fever
- Severe weakness
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe dehydration

Prompt treatment may save life.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Is tuberculosis contagious?

Yes.

Active pulmonary tuberculosis spreads through the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, talks, or sings.

---

## Can tuberculosis be cured?

Yes.

Most patients recover completely when diagnosed early and when the full course of treatment is completed.

---

## How long does TB treatment last?

Treatment usually lasts at least six months, although some patients may require longer treatment depending on the type of TB.

---

## Can TB come back after treatment?

Yes.

TB can return if treatment is not completed or if a person becomes infected again.

---

## Can children develop TB?

Yes.

Children can become infected, especially after close contact with adults who have active TB.

---

## Does everyone infected with TB become sick?

No.

Some people develop latent TB infection, where they carry the bacteria without symptoms or spreading the disease.

---

# Key Points

- Tuberculosis is caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*.
- It mainly affects the lungs but can affect other organs.
- A cough lasting more than two weeks should be medically evaluated.
- TB spreads through the air.
- GeneXpert helps diagnose TB quickly.
- Completing treatment prevents drug resistance.
- Tuberculosis is preventable and curable.
