# Medical Emergencies

## Overview

A medical emergency is a sudden illness or injury that requires immediate medical attention to prevent death, permanent disability, or serious complications.

Recognizing emergency warning signs early and seeking prompt treatment can save lives.

This guide provides general first-response information. It does not replace professional emergency medical care.

---

# What To Do During a Medical Emergency

If someone experiences a medical emergency:

- Stay calm.
- Ensure the area is safe.
- Check if the person is responsive.
- Call the local emergency medical service immediately.
- Do not leave the person alone unless necessary to get help.
- Follow instructions from emergency responders.
- Provide basic first aid if trained to do so.
- Do not give food or drinks to an unconscious person.

---

# Common Medical Emergencies

Examples include:

- Heart attack
- Stroke
- Severe bleeding
- Difficulty breathing
- Choking
- Seizures
- Severe burns
- Poisoning
- Electric shock
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe allergic reactions
- Major trauma

# Heart Attack

A heart attack occurs when blood flow to part of the heart is blocked.

## Warning Signs

- Chest pain or pressure
- Pain spreading to the arm, shoulder, neck, jaw, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Cold sweat
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Dizziness
- Extreme weakness

Symptoms may differ in women, older adults, and people with diabetes.

---

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Help the person sit in a comfortable position.
- Loosen tight clothing.
- Keep the person calm.
- Monitor breathing and responsiveness.
- If the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally, begin CPR if trained and if it is safe to do so while waiting for emergency responders.

Do not delay seeking medical care.

---

# Stroke

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted or when a blood vessel in the brain ruptures.

## Warning Signs (FAST)

**F – Face**

One side of the face droops.

**A – Arms**

One arm becomes weak or numb.

**S – Speech**

Speech becomes slurred or difficult.

**T – Time**

Call emergency medical services immediately.

Other symptoms include:

- Sudden confusion
- Sudden vision problems
- Sudden severe headache
- Loss of balance
- Difficulty walking

---

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Keep the person safe and comfortable.
- Do not give food or drinks if swallowing is impaired.
- Note the time symptoms began if known.
- Monitor breathing until help arrives.

Early treatment greatly improves outcomes.

---

# Severe Bleeding

Heavy bleeding can quickly become life-threatening.

## Signs

- Continuous bleeding
- Blood soaking through dressings
- Large pools of blood
- Pale skin
- Fast heartbeat
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Loss of consciousness

---

## What To Do

- Apply firm, direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage.
- Keep pressure on the wound until medical help arrives.
- If possible, elevate the injured limb above the level of the heart unless you suspect a fracture.
- Do not remove objects deeply embedded in the wound.
- Seek emergency medical care immediately.

---

# Difficulty Breathing

Difficulty breathing may result from:

- Asthma
- Pneumonia
- Severe allergic reactions
- Heart disease
- Airway obstruction

## Warning Signs

- Rapid breathing
- Wheezing
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Chest pain
- Inability to speak full sentences
- Severe shortness of breath

---

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Help the person sit upright if comfortable.
- Loosen tight clothing.
- If the person has a prescribed rescue inhaler, assist them in using it if they are able.
- Monitor breathing until professional help arrives.

---

# Loss of Consciousness

A person who is unconscious does not respond normally to voice or touch.

Possible causes include:

- Head injury
- Stroke
- Seizures
- Low blood sugar
- Poisoning
- Cardiac arrest

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Check for normal breathing.
- If breathing normally, place the person in the recovery position if there is no suspected spinal injury.
- Do not give food, drinks, or medicine by mouth.
- Monitor breathing until help arrives.

---

# Seizures

A seizure is caused by sudden abnormal electrical activity in the brain.

## During a Seizure

- Stay calm.
- Protect the person from nearby hazards.
- Place something soft under the head if possible.
- Loosen tight clothing around the neck.
- Time the seizure.

Do NOT:

- Hold the person down.
- Put anything into the person's mouth.
- Give food or drinks until they are fully awake.

Seek emergency medical care if:

- The seizure lasts more than five minutes.
- Repeated seizures occur without recovery.
- The person is injured.
- The person has difficulty breathing after the seizure.
- It is the person's first seizure.

---

# Choking

Choking occurs when an object blocks the airway, preventing normal breathing.

## Warning Signs

- Inability to speak
- Inability to cough
- Difficulty breathing
- High-pitched breathing sounds
- Clutching the throat
- Blue lips or face
- Loss of consciousness

## What To Do

If the person can cough or speak:

- Encourage them to continue coughing.

If the person cannot breathe, cough, or speak:

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- If you are trained, provide appropriate first aid for choking according to current guidelines.
- Continue until the object is expelled or emergency responders take over.

If the person becomes unconscious:

- Begin CPR if trained and it is safe to do so while waiting for emergency responders.

---

# Poisoning

Poisoning may occur through swallowing, inhalation, skin contact, or injection of harmful substances.

Common causes include:

- Medicines
- Household cleaning products
- Pesticides
- Carbon monoxide
- Chemicals
- Poisonous plants
- Food contamination

## Warning Signs

- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Difficulty breathing
- Confusion
- Burns around the mouth
- Seizures
- Unconsciousness

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services or your local poison information service immediately.
- Remove the person from further exposure if it is safe to do so.
- Keep the container or label of the suspected poison if available.
- Do **not** force vomiting unless specifically instructed by a qualified healthcare professional or poison centre.
- Follow professional instructions while waiting for help.

---

# Burns

Burns may be caused by:

- Fire
- Hot liquids
- Steam
- Chemicals
- Electricity
- Hot objects

## Types of Burns

- Superficial burns
- Partial-thickness burns
- Full-thickness burns

## What To Do

- Move the person away from the source of the burn if it is safe.
- Cool the burn with cool running water for at least 20 minutes as soon as possible.
- Remove rings, watches, or tight clothing before swelling develops if they are not stuck to the skin.
- Cover the burn with a clean, non-stick dressing or clean cloth.
- Seek urgent medical care for large, deep, electrical, chemical, facial, hand, foot, genital, or major joint burns.

Do **not**:

- Apply butter, oil, toothpaste, or traditional remedies.
- Break blisters.
- Apply ice directly to the burn.

---

# Electric Shock

Electric shock may cause serious internal injuries even when burns appear minor.

## What To Do

- Do not touch the person until the electrical source has been switched off or it is safe.
- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Check for breathing and responsiveness once the area is safe.
- Begin CPR if trained and the person is not breathing normally.
- All significant electrical injuries should be assessed by a healthcare professional.

---

# Heat Exhaustion

Heat exhaustion develops after excessive loss of water and salts due to heat exposure.

## Symptoms

- Heavy sweating
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Muscle cramps
- Headache
- Nausea
- Fast pulse

## What To Do

- Move the person to a cool place.
- Loosen or remove excess clothing.
- Cool the body with cool cloths or a fan.
- Encourage cool fluids if the person is fully alert and able to drink.
- Seek medical attention if symptoms do not improve quickly or worsen.

---

# Heat Stroke

Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency.

## Symptoms

- Very high body temperature
- Hot skin
- Confusion
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Move the person to a cool area.
- Begin rapid cooling using cool water, wet towels, or fans while waiting for help.
- Do not delay emergency care.

---

# Hypothermia

Hypothermia occurs when body temperature falls dangerously low.

## Symptoms

- Intense shivering
- Confusion
- Slurred speech
- Extreme drowsiness
- Slow breathing
- Loss of consciousness

## What To Do

- Move the person to a warm, dry place.
- Remove wet clothing.
- Cover with warm blankets.
- Warm the person gradually.
- Seek urgent medical care.

---

# Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

Anaphylaxis is a severe allergic reaction that can become fatal within minutes.

Common triggers include:

- Foods
- Medicines
- Insect stings
- Latex

## Warning Signs

- Swelling of the face or throat
- Difficulty breathing
- Wheezing
- Widespread rash or hives
- Dizziness
- Collapse

## What To Do

- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- If the person has been prescribed an epinephrine (adrenaline) auto-injector, assist them in using it according to their emergency action plan.
- Keep the person under close observation until help arrives.

---

# Snake Bite

Most snake bites require urgent medical assessment.

## What To Do

- Keep the person calm.
- Limit movement of the affected limb.
- Remove rings or tight jewellery before swelling develops.
- Seek emergency medical care immediately.

Do **not**:

- Cut the wound.
- Attempt to suck out venom.
- Apply ice.
- Apply harmful traditional substances.

---

# Dog and Animal Bites

Animal bites can lead to serious infection, including rabies in some regions.

## What To Do

- Wash the wound thoroughly with soap and running water for at least 15 minutes.
- Cover with a clean dressing.
- Seek medical care promptly for assessment, tetanus protection, antibiotics if needed, and rabies prevention when appropriate.

---

# Road Traffic Accidents

People involved in road traffic accidents may have hidden injuries.

## What To Do

- Ensure the scene is safe before approaching.
- Call emergency medical services.
- Do not move seriously injured people unless there is immediate danger (such as fire or explosion).
- Control severe bleeding if possible.
- Monitor breathing until help arrives.

---

# Drowning

A person rescued from water still requires urgent medical evaluation.

## What To Do

- Remove the person from the water only if it is safe.
- Call emergency medical services immediately.
- Check breathing and responsiveness.
- Begin CPR if trained and the person is not breathing normally.
- Keep the person warm while waiting for professional help.

Any person who has experienced a drowning incident should be evaluated by a healthcare professional, even if they appear to recover.

# Emergency Preparedness

Being prepared for emergencies can save lives and reduce the impact of sudden illness or injury.

Every household should:

- Know the location of the nearest healthcare facility.
- Keep important emergency telephone numbers readily available.
- Learn basic first aid and CPR where possible.
- Keep essential medicines in their original containers and out of the reach of children.
- Develop an emergency plan for family members.
- Ensure children know how to seek help from a trusted adult.

Preparation allows faster action during emergencies.

---

# Home Emergency Kit

A basic home emergency kit should be stored in a clean, dry, and easily accessible place.

It may include:

- Adhesive bandages
- Sterile gauze
- Medical tape
- Elastic bandage
- Antiseptic solution
- Disposable gloves
- Digital thermometer
- Scissors
- Tweezers
- Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)
- Prescribed emergency medicines
- Flashlight
- Spare batteries
- Emergency contact list
- Clean drinking water

Check supplies regularly and replace expired items.

---

# Calling for Emergency Help

When contacting emergency services:

Provide the following information clearly:

- Your name
- Exact location
- Telephone number
- What happened
- Number of injured or ill people
- Age of the patient if known
- Whether the person is conscious
- Whether the person is breathing

Remain calm and answer all questions from the emergency operator.

Do not hang up until instructed.

---

# Helping Children During Emergencies

Children may become frightened during emergencies.

Parents and caregivers should:

- Stay calm.
- Reassure the child.
- Keep the child warm.
- Monitor breathing.
- Do not give medicines unless advised by a healthcare professional.
- Seek immediate medical care if danger signs develop.

Children can deteriorate quickly, so prompt medical assessment is important.

---

# Helping Older Adults During Emergencies

Older adults may have chronic medical conditions that increase the risk of complications.

Special considerations include:

- Monitoring breathing carefully
- Checking for injuries after falls
- Bringing a list of current medications to the hospital if possible
- Informing healthcare workers about existing medical conditions
- Ensuring mobility aids and glasses accompany the patient when appropriate

Older adults should receive prompt medical evaluation after serious illness or injury.

---

# Preventing Medical Emergencies

Many emergencies can be prevented.

Important preventive measures include:

- Attend regular medical check-ups.
- Control high blood pressure and diabetes.
- Take medicines as prescribed.
- Wear seat belts while travelling.
- Use helmets when riding motorcycles or bicycles.
- Keep medicines and chemicals out of children's reach.
- Install smoke detectors where available.
- Practice safe cooking habits.
- Store fuels and chemicals safely.
- Learn basic first aid.

Prevention is one of the most effective ways to reduce injuries and medical emergencies.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## When should I call emergency medical services?

Call immediately if someone has:

- Difficulty breathing
- Chest pain suggestive of a heart attack
- Symptoms of stroke
- Severe bleeding
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures lasting more than five minutes
- Serious burns
- Severe allergic reactions
- Major injuries
- Suspected poisoning with severe symptoms

---

## Should I move an injured person?

Only move an injured person if there is immediate danger, such as fire, explosion, or another life-threatening hazard.

Otherwise, wait for trained emergency responders.

---

## Should I give food or water to an unconscious person?

No.

Never give food, drinks, or medicines by mouth to an unconscious person because of the risk of choking.

---

## Should I remove an object stuck in a deep wound?

No.

Do not remove deeply embedded objects. They may be helping to reduce bleeding.

Seek emergency medical care immediately.

---

## What should I do after someone recovers from choking or drowning?

Even if the person appears well, they should be assessed by a healthcare professional because delayed complications can occur.

# Key Points

- Medical emergencies require immediate action.
- Stay calm and ensure the scene is safe.
- Call emergency medical services without delay.
- Recognize warning signs of heart attack, stroke, severe bleeding, choking, poisoning, and difficulty breathing.
- Learn basic first aid and CPR if possible.
- Keep a well-stocked home emergency kit.
- Never delay seeking professional medical care during a serious emergency.
