# Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) and Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS)

## Overview

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) is a virus that attacks the body's immune system, specifically CD4 (T-helper) cells. If left untreated, HIV weakens the immune system over time, making it difficult for the body to fight infections and certain cancers.

Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is the most advanced stage of HIV infection. Not everyone living with HIV develops AIDS. With early diagnosis and consistent treatment using Antiretroviral Therapy (ART), many people with HIV live long, healthy, and productive lives.

HIV remains a significant public health concern worldwide, but modern treatment has transformed it into a manageable chronic condition.

---

# Difference Between HIV and AIDS

## HIV

- HIV is the virus that infects the body.
- It attacks the immune system.
- It can be controlled with lifelong treatment.
- A person with HIV may appear healthy for many years.

## AIDS

- AIDS is the most advanced stage of HIV infection.
- It occurs when the immune system becomes severely weakened.
- People with AIDS are at high risk of serious infections and certain cancers.

Not every person with HIV develops AIDS, especially if treatment begins early.

---

# Causes

HIV is caused by the Human Immunodeficiency Virus.

The virus attacks and gradually destroys CD4 immune cells, reducing the body's ability to fight infections.

---

# How HIV Spreads

HIV can be transmitted through:

- Unprotected vaginal sex
- Unprotected anal sex
- Sharing contaminated needles or syringes
- Transfusion of infected blood (rare where blood is screened)
- Mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding
- Occupational exposure through contaminated needles (healthcare workers)

Only specific body fluids transmit HIV:

- Blood
- Semen
- Vaginal fluids
- Rectal fluids
- Breast milk

---

# HIV Does NOT Spread Through

HIV cannot be transmitted by:

- Handshakes
- Hugging
- Sharing food
- Sharing drinking cups
- Sharing toilets
- Mosquito bites
- Sweat
- Tears
- Saliva during casual contact
- Coughing or sneezing

People living with HIV should not face discrimination because HIV is not spread through everyday social contact.

---

# Risk Factors

Higher risk occurs in people who:

- Have unprotected sex
- Have multiple sexual partners
- Share needles
- Have another sexually transmitted infection (STI)
- Receive unscreened blood
- Have occupational exposure to infected blood

---

# Stages of HIV Infection

## Stage 1: Acute HIV Infection

This occurs within weeks after infection.

Symptoms may include:

- Fever
- Rash
- Sore throat
- Swollen lymph nodes
- Muscle pain
- Headache
- Fatigue
- Mouth ulcers

Some people have no symptoms.

---

## Stage 2: Chronic HIV Infection

This stage may last for many years.

The virus remains active but often causes few or no symptoms.

Without treatment, HIV continues damaging the immune system.

---

## Stage 3: AIDS

This is the advanced stage of HIV infection.

The immune system becomes severely weakened.

People become vulnerable to opportunistic infections and certain cancers.

---

# Common Symptoms

Symptoms vary depending on the stage.

Possible symptoms include:

- Fever
- Fatigue
- Swollen lymph nodes
- Night sweats
- Weight loss
- Persistent diarrhea
- Persistent cough
- Mouth sores
- Skin rashes
- Frequent infections

---

# Symptoms of Advanced HIV/AIDS

Seek medical evaluation if symptoms include:

- Significant unexplained weight loss
- Persistent fever
- Night sweats
- Severe diarrhea
- Chronic cough
- Difficulty breathing
- Recurrent pneumonia
- Tuberculosis
- Oral thrush
- Severe weakness
- Confusion
- Seizures

---

# Opportunistic Infections

People with advanced HIV may develop:

- Tuberculosis (TB)
- Pneumonia
- Oral candidiasis (thrush)
- Cryptococcal meningitis
- Toxoplasmosis
- Cytomegalovirus infection
- Chronic diarrhea
- Kaposi sarcoma
- Cervical cancer
- Other serious infections

Early ART greatly reduces these risks.

---

# Diagnosis

Healthcare professionals diagnose HIV using:

- Rapid HIV test
- HIV antibody test
- HIV antigen/antibody test
- HIV PCR test (especially for infants)
- Viral load test
- CD4 cell count

Positive screening tests should be confirmed according to national testing guidelines.

---

# Treatment

There is currently no cure for HIV.

However, HIV can be effectively controlled using Antiretroviral Therapy (ART).

Treatment aims to:

- Reduce viral load
- Protect the immune system
- Prevent progression to AIDS
- Improve quality of life
- Reduce transmission to others

Treatment should begin as soon as possible after diagnosis.

Patients should:

- Take medicines every day
- Never skip doses
- Attend regular clinic appointments
- Monitor viral load and CD4 count

Good adherence is essential for successful treatment.

---

# Living Well with HIV

People living with HIV can lead healthy lives by:

- Taking ART consistently
- Eating a balanced diet
- Exercising regularly
- Getting enough sleep
- Avoiding smoking
- Limiting alcohol
- Managing stress
- Attending routine medical appointments
- Receiving recommended vaccinations

Mental health support is also important.

---

# Prevention

HIV prevention includes:

- Correct and consistent condom use
- HIV testing
- Mutual monogamy
- Avoiding sharing needles
- Safe blood transfusion
- Medical male circumcision where appropriate
- Prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT)
- Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP) for people at high risk
- Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) after possible exposure
- Early ART for people living with HIV

People with an undetectable viral load through consistent ART greatly reduce the risk of transmitting HIV to sexual partners.

---

# HIV During Pregnancy

Pregnant women living with HIV can significantly reduce the risk of transmitting HIV to their babies by:

- Starting ART early
- Attending antenatal care
- Following medical advice during delivery
- Receiving appropriate care for the newborn

With proper treatment, many babies are born HIV-free.

---

# Possible Complications

Without treatment, HIV may lead to:

- AIDS
- Tuberculosis
- Pneumonia
- Chronic infections
- Severe weight loss
- Neurological disorders
- Certain cancers
- Kidney disease
- Liver disease
- Death

Early treatment greatly reduces these complications.

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Seek immediate medical attention if a person with HIV develops:

- Difficulty breathing
- Persistent high fever
- Severe chest pain
- Confusion
- Seizures
- Severe dehydration
- Loss of consciousness
- Persistent vomiting
- Severe weakness
- Signs of meningitis

These symptoms may indicate a serious infection requiring urgent treatment.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Can HIV be cured?

Currently, there is no cure for HIV. However, ART allows most people to live long and healthy lives.

---

## Can HIV be prevented?

Yes.

Correct condom use, HIV testing, PrEP, PEP, avoiding shared needles, and early treatment all reduce the risk of infection.

---

## Can someone with HIV have children?

Yes.

With proper medical care and ART, many people living with HIV have healthy pregnancies and HIV-negative babies.

---

## Can mosquitoes spread HIV?

No.

Mosquitoes and other insects do not transmit HIV.

---

## Can HIV spread through kissing?

Casual kissing does not spread HIV. HIV is not transmitted through saliva during normal social contact.

---

## Why is regular treatment important?

Taking ART every day keeps the viral load low, protects the immune system, improves health, and greatly reduces the chance of transmitting HIV.

---

# Key Points

- HIV attacks the immune system.
- AIDS is the advanced stage of untreated HIV infection.
- HIV spreads through certain body fluids, not through casual contact.
- Early testing and treatment improve health outcomes.
- ART allows many people to live long and productive lives.
- HIV prevention includes condoms, PrEP, PEP, safe blood, and avoiding shared needles.
- Pregnant women living with HIV can reduce transmission to their babies with proper medical care.
