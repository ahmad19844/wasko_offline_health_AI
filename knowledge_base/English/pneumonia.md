# Pneumonia

## Overview

Pneumonia is an infection that inflames one or both lungs, causing the air sacs (alveoli) to fill with fluid or pus. This reduces the lungs' ability to deliver oxygen to the body and can make breathing difficult.

Pneumonia is a major cause of illness and death worldwide, especially among young children, older adults, and people with weakened immune systems. It may be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or, less commonly, aspiration of food or liquids into the lungs.

Early diagnosis and appropriate treatment greatly improve recovery.

---

# Causes

Pneumonia can be caused by several microorganisms, including:

## Bacterial Causes

- Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common)
- Haemophilus influenzae
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Klebsiella pneumoniae
- Mycoplasma pneumoniae

## Viral Causes

- Influenza virus
- Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)
- Coronavirus (including COVID-19)
- Adenovirus

## Fungal Causes

Fungal pneumonia mainly affects people with weakened immune systems.

Common fungi include:

- Pneumocystis jirovecii
- Histoplasma
- Cryptococcus

## Aspiration Pneumonia

Occurs when food, drinks, saliva, or vomit enter the lungs.

---

# Risk Factors

The risk of pneumonia increases in:

- Children under five years
- Adults aged 65 years and above
- Smokers
- People living with HIV/AIDS
- Patients with diabetes
- People with chronic lung diseases
- Individuals with asthma
- Patients with heart disease
- Malnourished individuals
- Cancer patients
- Hospitalized patients
- People with weakened immune systems

---

# Types of Pneumonia

## Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

Develops outside healthcare facilities.

---

## Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP)

Develops 48 hours or more after hospital admission.

---

## Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

Occurs in patients receiving mechanical ventilation.

---

## Aspiration Pneumonia

Caused by inhaling food, liquid, or stomach contents into the lungs.

---

# Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms may develop suddenly or gradually.

Common symptoms include:

- Persistent cough
- Cough producing mucus or sputum
- Fever
- Chills
- Shortness of breath
- Chest pain that worsens during breathing or coughing
- Rapid breathing
- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Sweating
- Loss of appetite
- Headache
- Muscle aches

Children may also experience:

- Fast breathing
- Poor feeding
- Irritability
- Grunting
- Bluish lips in severe cases

Older adults may present with:

- Confusion
- Reduced alertness
- Weakness
- Poor appetite

---

# Severe Symptoms

Seek emergency medical care immediately if you experience:

- Severe difficulty breathing
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Severe chest pain
- Confusion
- Persistent high fever
- Oxygen saturation below normal (if available)
- Rapid worsening of symptoms
- Inability to drink fluids
- Persistent vomiting
- Loss of consciousness

These symptoms require urgent medical evaluation.

---

# Possible Complications

Untreated pneumonia may result in:

- Respiratory failure
- Sepsis
- Lung abscess
- Pleural effusion (fluid around the lungs)
- Empyema (pus around the lungs)
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
- Kidney failure
- Heart complications
- Death

Prompt treatment greatly reduces these risks.

---

# Diagnosis

Healthcare professionals may diagnose pneumonia using:

- Medical history
- Physical examination
- Chest X-ray
- Blood tests
- Pulse oximetry
- Sputum examination
- Blood cultures
- CT scan (when necessary)
- Viral testing during outbreaks

---

# Treatment

Treatment depends on the cause and severity.

## Bacterial Pneumonia

May be treated with prescribed antibiotics.

## Viral Pneumonia

Treatment may include:

- Rest
- Adequate fluids
- Fever management
- Antiviral medicines in selected cases

## Fungal Pneumonia

Requires antifungal medicines prescribed by a healthcare professional.

Supportive treatment may include:

- Oxygen therapy
- Intravenous fluids
- Fever-reducing medicines
- Hospital admission for severe illness

Patients should always complete prescribed medications.

---

# Home Care

Patients recovering at home should:

- Get adequate rest
- Drink plenty of clean fluids
- Take prescribed medicines correctly
- Eat nutritious meals
- Avoid smoking
- Avoid alcohol
- Monitor symptoms
- Return for medical review if symptoms worsen

Do not stop antibiotics early unless advised by a healthcare professional.

---

# Prevention

Pneumonia can often be prevented by:

- Receiving recommended vaccinations
- Washing hands regularly
- Avoiding smoking
- Maintaining good nutrition
- Covering the mouth when coughing
- Wearing a mask when appropriate
- Managing chronic diseases
- Breastfeeding infants
- Avoiding close contact with sick individuals

Vaccines that help reduce the risk include:

- Pneumococcal vaccine
- Influenza vaccine
- COVID-19 vaccine (where appropriate)
- Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) vaccine

---

# Nutrition During Recovery

A healthy diet supports healing.

Recommended foods include:

- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Whole grains
- Beans
- Eggs
- Fish
- Lean meat
- Milk and dairy products
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Small, frequent meals may be helpful for patients with reduced appetite.

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the nearest hospital immediately if the patient develops:

- Severe shortness of breath
- Chest pain
- Blue lips or fingertips
- Confusion
- Persistent high fever
- Severe weakness
- Oxygen saturation below 90% (if measured)
- Persistent vomiting
- Difficulty staying awake
- Loss of consciousness

Children with rapid breathing or difficulty feeding should also receive immediate medical attention.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Is pneumonia contagious?

Some types of pneumonia caused by bacteria or viruses can spread from person to person through respiratory droplets. Good hygiene helps reduce transmission.

---

## Can pneumonia be cured?

Yes.

Most patients recover completely with early diagnosis and appropriate treatment.

---

## Is pneumonia the same as a common cold?

No.

A common cold usually affects the upper respiratory tract, while pneumonia is an infection of the lungs and is generally much more serious.

---

## Who is most at risk?

Young children, older adults, pregnant women, people with chronic diseases, and those with weakened immune systems are at higher risk of severe pneumonia.

---

## Can pneumonia be prevented?

Yes.

Vaccination, handwashing, avoiding smoking, good nutrition, and prompt treatment of respiratory infections all help reduce the risk.

---

# Key Points

- Pneumonia is an infection of the lungs.
- It may be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or aspiration.
- Common symptoms include cough, fever, chest pain, and difficulty breathing.
- Young children and older adults are at greater risk of severe illness.
- Early diagnosis and treatment improve recovery.
- Vaccination and good hygiene help prevent pneumonia.
