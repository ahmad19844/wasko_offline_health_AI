# Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

## Overview

Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a long-term medical condition in which the force of blood against the walls of the arteries remains consistently higher than normal. It is one of the leading causes of heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, and premature death worldwide.

Hypertension is often called the **"silent killer"** because many people have no symptoms until serious complications develop. Regular blood pressure checks are important for early detection and treatment.

With healthy lifestyle changes and appropriate medication when prescribed, hypertension can be effectively controlled.

---

# What is Blood Pressure?

Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of the arteries.

It is recorded using two numbers:

- **Systolic pressure:** The pressure when the heart contracts.
- **Diastolic pressure:** The pressure when the heart relaxes between beats.

Example:

120/80 mmHg

Normal blood pressure is generally considered to be **below 120/80 mmHg**. Blood pressure categories and treatment decisions should always follow current clinical guidelines and be interpreted by a healthcare professional.

---

# Causes

Most adults have **Primary (Essential) Hypertension**, which has no single identifiable cause and develops gradually over time.

Some people have **Secondary Hypertension**, which results from another medical condition or medication.

Possible causes include:

- Kidney disease
- Hormonal disorders
- Certain medications
- Obstructive sleep apnea
- Congenital blood vessel abnormalities

---

# Risk Factors

The risk of hypertension increases with:

- Increasing age
- Family history of hypertension
- Overweight or obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Excess dietary salt
- Low intake of fruits and vegetables
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Diabetes
- Kidney disease
- High cholesterol
- Chronic stress

---

# Signs and Symptoms

Most people with hypertension have **no symptoms**.

When symptoms occur, especially with very high blood pressure, they may include:

- Persistent headache
- Dizziness
- Blurred vision
- Nosebleeds
- Chest discomfort
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue
- Palpitations (awareness of heartbeat)

Symptoms alone cannot confirm hypertension. Blood pressure should be measured using appropriate equipment.

---

# Severe Symptoms

Seek emergency medical care immediately if a person with high blood pressure develops:

- Severe chest pain
- Sudden weakness or numbness of the face, arm, or leg
- Difficulty speaking
- Sudden loss of vision
- Severe shortness of breath
- Confusion
- Seizures
- Severe headache with vomiting
- Loss of consciousness

These symptoms may indicate a hypertensive emergency, stroke, or heart attack.

---

# Possible Complications

If left untreated, hypertension may lead to:

- Stroke
- Heart attack
- Heart failure
- Kidney disease
- Kidney failure
- Vision loss
- Peripheral artery disease
- Aortic aneurysm
- Cognitive decline

Proper treatment greatly reduces these risks.

---

# Diagnosis

Healthcare professionals diagnose hypertension by measuring blood pressure using a validated blood pressure monitor.

Additional tests may include:

- Repeated blood pressure measurements
- Home blood pressure monitoring
- Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring
- Blood tests
- Urine tests
- Kidney function tests
- Blood sugar testing
- Cholesterol testing
- Electrocardiogram (ECG)
- Echocardiogram (when indicated)

Diagnosis should not be based on a single reading unless clinically necessary.

---

# Treatment

Treatment depends on the blood pressure level, overall health, and presence of other medical conditions.

Treatment may include:

- Healthy diet
- Regular physical activity
- Weight reduction when appropriate
- Reducing salt intake
- Stopping smoking
- Limiting alcohol
- Stress management
- Blood pressure-lowering medicines prescribed by a healthcare professional

Common classes of medicines include:

- Diuretics
- ACE inhibitors
- Angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Beta-blockers

Patients should take medicines exactly as prescribed and should not stop treatment without medical advice.

# Lifestyle Changes

Healthy lifestyle habits help control blood pressure.

Recommendations include:

- Eat more fruits and vegetables
- Reduce salt intake
- Choose whole grains
- Limit processed foods
- Exercise regularly
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Avoid smoking
- Limit alcohol intake
- Sleep adequately
- Manage stress

Lifestyle changes often improve overall cardiovascular health.

---

# Home Care

People living with hypertension should:

- Monitor blood pressure regularly
- Take prescribed medicines consistently
- Attend follow-up appointments
- Eat a balanced diet
- Stay physically active
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Avoid tobacco products
- Limit alcohol
- Learn the warning signs of complications

Do not stop medication because you feel well. Hypertension often has no symptoms.

---

# Prevention

The risk of hypertension can be reduced by:

- Maintaining a healthy weight
- Eating a balanced diet
- Limiting salt intake
- Exercising regularly
- Avoiding tobacco
- Limiting alcohol
- Managing diabetes
- Managing cholesterol
- Reducing stress
- Having regular health check-ups

Early detection is one of the best forms of prevention.

Go to the nearest hospital immediately if the patient develops:

- Severe chest pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Sudden weakness
- Difficulty speaking
- Severe headache with confusion
- Vision loss
- Seizures
- Fainting
- Loss of consciousness

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Can hypertension be cured?

Primary hypertension usually cannot be cured, but it can be effectively controlled with healthy lifestyle changes and medication when prescribed.

---

## Why is hypertension called the silent killer?

Many people have no symptoms even when blood pressure is dangerously high. Damage to the heart, brain, kidneys, and eyes may occur before symptoms appear.

---

## Can young people develop hypertension?

Yes.

Although more common in older adults, hypertension can affect younger adults and even children.

---

## Should I stop taking my medicine if my blood pressure becomes normal?

No.

Blood pressure often remains normal because the treatment is working. Never stop medication without consulting your healthcare provider.

---

## Does eating too much salt increase blood pressure?

High salt intake can contribute to high blood pressure in many people. Reducing salt intake is an important lifestyle measure.

# Key Points

- Hypertension is often called the "silent killer."
