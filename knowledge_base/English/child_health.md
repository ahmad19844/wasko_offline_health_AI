# Child Health

## Overview

Child health focuses on the physical, mental, emotional, and social well-being of children from birth through adolescence. Healthy children are more likely to grow into healthy adults, making early healthcare, proper nutrition, immunization, and disease prevention essential.

The first five years of life are especially important because rapid growth and brain development occur during this period. Parents and caregivers play a major role in ensuring children receive adequate nutrition, healthcare, emotional support, and protection from preventable diseases.

---

# Goals of Child Healthcare

Child healthcare aims to:

- Promote healthy growth and development
- Prevent childhood illnesses
- Detect diseases early
- Ensure complete immunization
- Support proper nutrition
- Reduce child mortality
- Promote healthy development
- Educate parents and caregivers

---

# Newborn Care

A newborn is a baby from birth to 28 days of age.

The first month of life is the most delicate period, requiring careful monitoring and proper care.

Essential newborn care includes:

- Keeping the baby warm
- Early initiation of breastfeeding
- Exclusive breastfeeding
- Proper cord care
- Good hygiene
- Immunization at birth
- Monitoring for danger signs

---

# Immediate Care After Birth

Immediately after delivery, healthcare providers should:

- Dry the baby thoroughly
- Keep the baby warm
- Assess breathing
- Clamp and cut the umbilical cord safely
- Encourage skin-to-skin contact
- Initiate breastfeeding within one hour of birth

Early breastfeeding helps provide protective antibodies through colostrum.

---

# Breastfeeding

Breast milk provides the ideal nutrition for infants.

Exclusive breastfeeding means giving only breast milk for the first six months of life, unless medically indicated otherwise.

No additional water, herbal preparations, or other foods are usually needed during this period.

---

# Benefits of Breastfeeding

## Benefits for the Baby

- Complete nutrition
- Protection against infections
- Reduced diarrhea
- Reduced pneumonia
- Healthy brain development
- Strong immune system
- Lower risk of malnutrition

## Benefits for the Mother

- Faster recovery after childbirth
- Reduced postpartum bleeding
- Strong mother–baby bonding
- Lower risk of breast cancer
- Lower risk of ovarian cancer

---

# Complementary Feeding

From six months of age, breast milk alone is no longer sufficient to meet all nutritional needs.

Complementary foods should be introduced while breastfeeding continues.

Healthy complementary foods include:

- Soft porridge
- Mashed potatoes
- Beans
- Eggs
- Fish
- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Rice
- Millet
- Maize

Meals should be clean, nutritious, and appropriate for the child's age.

# Growth and Development

Healthy growth is monitored using:

- Weight
- Length or height
- Head circumference
- Developmental milestones

Regular clinic visits help identify growth problems early.

---

# Child Nutrition

Good nutrition is essential for healthy growth, brain development, strong immunity, and disease prevention.

Children should receive a balanced diet containing foods from all major food groups.

Healthy foods include:

- Breast milk for infants
- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Whole grains
- Beans
- Eggs
- Fish
- Lean meat
- Milk and dairy products
- Groundnuts (for children old enough to safely eat them)
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Limit foods and drinks high in sugar, salt, and unhealthy fats.

# Common Childhood Illnesses

Children commonly experience illnesses such as:

- Malaria
- Pneumonia
- Diarrhea
- Measles
- Ear infections
- Worm infestations
- Skin infections
- Common cold
- Influenza

Early diagnosis and treatment reduce complications.

# Fever

Fever is a common symptom in children.

Possible causes include:

- Malaria
- Viral infections
- Pneumonia
- Ear infection
- Urinary tract infection
- Measles

Parents should:

- Encourage fluids
- Dress the child lightly
- Monitor temperature
- Seek medical advice if fever persists or the child appears seriously ill

---

# Diarrhea

Diarrhea is the passage of loose or watery stools several times a day.

Common causes include:

- Viral infections
- Bacterial infections
- Contaminated food
[O- Unsafe drinking water
- Poor hygiene

Children can become dehydrated quickly.

---

# Signs of Dehydration

Watch for:

- Dry mouth
- Sunken eyes
- Little or no urine
- Extreme thirst
- Lethargy
- Irritability
- Poor skin elasticity
- Fast heartbeat

Severe dehydration is a medical emergency.

---

# Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

ORS replaces water and salts lost during diarrhea.

Parents should:

- Begin ORS as soon as diarrhea starts.
- Continue breastfeeding.
- Continue feeding age-appropriate foods.
- Offer small, frequent sips.

Children with severe dehydration require immediate hospital care.

---

# Zinc Supplementation

Zinc helps reduce:

- Duration of diarrhea
- Severity of diarrhea
- Future diarrheal episodes

Healthcare providers may recommend zinc supplements during episodes of acute diarrhea.

---

# Malaria in Children

Children under five years are at increased risk of severe malaria.

Common symptoms include:

- Fever
- Chills
- Vomiting
- Poor feeding
- Weakness
- Convulsions
- Sleepiness

Malaria should be diagnosed and treated promptly.

Use insecticide-treated mosquito nets to reduce mosquito bites.

---

# Pneumonia in Children

Pneumonia is a serious lung infection.

Symptoms include:

- Fast breathing
- Difficulty breathing
- Chest indrawing
- Fever
- Persistent cough
- Poor feeding

Children with breathing difficulty should be taken to the nearest healthcare facility immediately.

---

# Worm Infestations

Intestinal worms may affect children's growth and nutrition.

Symptoms include:

- Abdominal pain
- Poor appetite
- Weight loss
- Anemia
- Poor growth

Preventive measures include:

- Hand washing
- Safe drinking water
- Proper sanitation
- Regular deworming according to local health recommendations

---

# Skin Infections

Children may develop skin infections caused by bacteria, fungi, parasites, or viruses.

Common conditions include:

- Ringworm
- Impetigo
- Scabies
- Boils

Good hygiene and early treatment help prevent spread.

---

# Oral Health

Healthy teeth are important for nutrition and speech.

Parents should:

- Clean infants' gums gently.
- Begin brushing when teeth appear.
- Use age-appropriate toothpaste.
- Limit sugary foods and drinks.
- Attend regular dental check-ups.

---

# Hygiene and Sanitation

Good hygiene prevents many childhood illnesses.

Teach children to:

- Wash hands with soap before eating.
- Wash hands after using the toilet.
- Drink safe water.
- Use clean toilets.
- Keep fingernails short and clean.
- Bathe regularly.

Caregivers should prepare food hygienically.

---

# Injury Prevention

Many childhood injuries can be prevented.

Parents should:

- Keep medicines out of children's reach.
- Store chemicals safely.
- Supervise children near water.
- Use child safety seats where available.
- Prevent burns from hot liquids.
- Cover electrical outlets.
- Keep sharp objects safely stored.
- Prevent falls from stairs and balconies.

Creating a safe home environment greatly reduces accidental injuries.

---

- Love and affection
- A safe home environment
- Positive encouragement
- Opportunities to play
- Age-appropriate learning activities

Children learn through play, communication, and daily experiences.

# Sleep Requirements

Adequate sleep supports healthy growth and brain development.

General recommendations include:

### Newborns (0–3 months)

- 14–17 hours daily

### Infants (4–12 months)

- 12–16 hours daily (including naps)

### Toddlers (1–2 years)

- 11–14 hours daily

### Preschool Children (3–5 years)

- 10–13 hours daily

Sleep needs vary from child to child.

Regular physical activity helps children:

- Build strong bones and muscles
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Improve heart health
- Develop coordination
- Improve mood
- Sleep better

Encourage active play every day and reduce prolonged screen time.

- Monitor growth
- Assess development
- Receive scheduled immunizations

# Protecting Children from Disease

Parents and caregivers can reduce illness by:

- Completing all recommended vaccinations
- Exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months
- Providing nutritious meals
- Practicing good hygiene
- Using insecticide-treated mosquito nets
- Providing safe drinking water
- Maintaining clean surroundings
- Seeking prompt medical care when children become ill

---

# Child Safety

Protect children by:

- Supervising young children at all times
- Keeping medicines locked away
- Preventing access to cleaning chemicals
- Securing sharp objects
- Preventing drowning
- Using appropriate child restraints in vehicles
- Preventing burns and electrical injuries
- Teaching road safety as children grow older

---

# Danger Signs in Children

Seek immediate medical attention if a child develops:

- Difficulty breathing
- Fast breathing
- Severe chest indrawing
- Convulsions
- Loss of consciousness
- Persistent high fever
- Repeated vomiting
- Refusal to feed
- Severe dehydration
- Blue lips or fingers
- Severe weakness
- Persistent diarrhea with blood
- Stiff neck
- Severe burns
- Poisoning

These symptoms may indicate life-threatening illness.

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the nearest healthcare facility immediately if a child:

- Has difficulty breathing
- Has seizures
- Is unconscious
- Cannot drink or breastfeed
- Has persistent vomiting
- Has severe dehydration
- Develops heavy bleeding
- Has a high fever with confusion
- Shows signs of severe allergic reaction
- Is involved in a serious accident

Do not delay seeking medical care.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## How often should children receive vaccinations?

Children should receive vaccines according to the national immunization schedule. Parents should keep an up-to-date immunization record and attend all scheduled clinic visits.

---

## Is exclusive breastfeeding enough for the first six months?

Yes.

For most healthy infants, breast milk alone provides all the nutrients and fluids needed during the first six months of life unless a healthcare professional advises otherwise.

---

## When should complementary foods begin?

Complementary foods should generally be introduced at around six months of age while breastfeeding continues.

---

## What should I do if my child has diarrhea?

Continue breastfeeding or feeding, begin Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS), and seek medical care if the child becomes dehydrated, has blood in the stool, persistent vomiting, or appears seriously ill.

---

## How can I prevent malaria in my child?

Use insecticide-treated mosquito nets, eliminate mosquito breeding sites, dress children in protective clothing when appropriate, and seek prompt testing and treatment if fever develops.

---

## What should I do if my child has a fever?

Encourage fluids, monitor the child's condition, and seek medical attention if the fever is persistent, very high, or accompanied by danger signs such as seizures, difficulty breathing, or poor feeding.
