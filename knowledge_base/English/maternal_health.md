# Maternal Health

## Overview

Maternal health refers to the health of women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postnatal period. Good maternal health ensures that mothers receive quality healthcare before, during, and after delivery to reduce illness and prevent maternal deaths.

Every pregnancy carries some level of risk, but many complications can be prevented through early antenatal care, skilled birth attendance, timely treatment of complications, proper nutrition, and good family support.

Improving maternal health also improves newborn survival, child development, and the overall well-being of families and communities.

---

# Objectives of Maternal Healthcare

Maternal healthcare aims to:

- Promote healthy pregnancies
- Prevent pregnancy complications
- Detect health problems early
- Ensure safe childbirth
- Improve newborn survival
- Reduce maternal deaths
- Support healthy breastfeeding
- Promote family planning
- Improve women's overall health

---

## Antenatal Care

Healthcare received during pregnancy.

## Intrapartum Care

Healthcare provided during labour and delivery.

---

## Postnatal Care

Healthcare provided after childbirth for both mother and baby.

All three stages are equally important.

- Reduce newborn deaths

# Antenatal Care

Antenatal care (ANC) is regular medical care during pregnancy.

Women should attend their first antenatal visit as early as possible after pregnancy is confirmed.

Routine antenatal care allows healthcare providers to:

- Monitor maternal health
- Monitor fetal growth
- Detect complications
- Treat infections
- Monitor blood pressure
- Provide nutrition counselling
- Provide health education
- Prepare women for childbirth

Regular attendance greatly improves pregnancy outcomes.

---

# What Happens During Antenatal Visits?

Healthcare workers may:

- Measure blood pressure
- Measure weight
- Check fetal growth
- Listen to the baby's heartbeat
- Test urine
- Perform blood tests
- Screen for infections
- Give vaccinations
- Prescribe supplements
- Provide counselling

Additional investigations may be performed depending on the mother's health.

# Essential Nutritional Care

Proper nutrition reduces pregnancy complications.

Important nutrients include:

## Iron

Helps prevent anemia.

Sources include:

- Meat
- Beans
- Green vegetables
- Fortified cereals

---

## Folic Acid

Reduces the risk of neural tube defects.

Sources include:

- Leafy vegetables
- Beans
- Fortified grains

Supplements are commonly recommended before conception and during early pregnancy.

---

## Calcium

Supports healthy bones and teeth.

Sources include:

- Milk
- Yogurt
- Cheese
- Sardines
- Green vegetables

---

## Protein

Supports fetal growth.

Sources include:

- Eggs
- Fish
- Chicken
- Beans
- Soybeans
- Lean meat

---

## Clean Water

Adequate hydration supports:

- Blood circulation
- Kidney function
- Amniotic fluid balance
- Temperature regulation

Pregnant women should drink sufficient clean water every day.

# Micronutrient Supplementation

Healthcare providers may prescribe:

- Iron tablets
- Folic acid
- Calcium supplements
- Multiple micronutrient supplements

These supplements should only be taken as directed.

---

# Maternal Immunization

Vaccination protects both mother and baby.

Recommended vaccines may include:

- Tetanus-containing vaccine
- Influenza vaccine
- COVID-19 vaccine where appropriate

Vaccination schedules should follow national guidelines.

# Healthy Lifestyle During Pregnancy

Pregnant women should:

- Eat balanced meals
- Exercise moderately
- Get enough sleep
- Avoid smoking
- Avoid alcohol
- Avoid recreational drugs
- Reduce stress
- Attend clinic appointments
- Take prescribed medicines only

Healthy habits improve maternal and fetal health.

# Common Pregnancy Discomforts

Normal discomforts include:

- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Heartburn
- Back pain
- Constipation
- Swollen feet
- Fatigue
- Frequent urination
- Leg cramps

Persistent or severe symptoms should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

---

# High-Risk Pregnancy

Some pregnancies require closer medical supervision because of increased risks to the mother, baby, or both.

A pregnancy may be considered high-risk if the mother has:

- Age below 18 years
- Age above 35 years
- High blood pressure
- Diabetes mellitus
- Heart disease
- Kidney disease
- HIV infection
- Severe anemia
- Obesity
- Multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets, or more)
- Previous miscarriage
- Previous premature birth
- Previous Caesarean section
- Previous stillbirth
- History of postpartum hemorrhage

Women with high-risk pregnancies should attend all scheduled antenatal appointments and deliver in a healthcare facility with skilled healthcare professionals.

# Common Maternal Complications

Complications during pregnancy and childbirth may become life-threatening if not treated promptly.

Common maternal complications include:

- Severe bleeding
- High blood pressure disorders
- Gestational diabetes
- Maternal infections
- Obstructed labour
- Premature labour
- Placental complications
- Severe anemia
- Blood clots

Early diagnosis greatly improves outcomes.

---

# Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

High blood pressure during pregnancy can threaten both the mother and the baby.

Conditions include:

- Gestational hypertension
- Pre-eclampsia
- Eclampsia

Regular blood pressure monitoring is essential throughout pregnancy.

---

# Pre-eclampsia

Pre-eclampsia is a serious condition characterized by high blood pressure and signs of organ damage after 20 weeks of pregnancy.

Possible symptoms include:

- Severe headache
- Blurred vision
- Swelling of the face or hands
- Pain in the upper abdomen
- Nausea or vomiting
- Reduced urine output
- Sudden weight gain

Without treatment, pre-eclampsia may progress to eclampsia.

---

# Eclampsia

Eclampsia is a medical emergency in which a pregnant woman with pre-eclampsia develops seizures.

Symptoms may include:

- Convulsions
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe headache
- Vision problems
- Confusion

Immediate emergency medical treatment is required.

---

# Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes develops during pregnancy when blood sugar levels become higher than normal.

Risk factors include:

- Overweight or obesity
- Family history of diabetes
- Previous gestational diabetes
- Previous large baby
- Advanced maternal age

Management may include:

- Healthy eating
- Physical activity
- Blood sugar monitoring
- Medication or insulin when necessary

Blood sugar usually returns to normal after delivery, but future monitoring is important.

---

# Maternal Anemia

Anemia is common during pregnancy.

Causes include:

- Iron deficiency
- Poor nutrition
- Malaria
- Hookworm infection
- Blood loss

Symptoms include:

- Fatigue
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Pale skin
- Shortness of breath

Treatment may include iron supplements, folic acid, treatment of underlying causes, and improved nutrition.

---

# Maternal Infections

Pregnant women are more vulnerable to certain infections.

Important infections include:

- Malaria
- Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
- HIV
- Hepatitis B
- Syphilis
- Tuberculosis

Early screening and treatment help protect both mother and baby.

---

# Postpartum Hemorrhage

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is excessive bleeding after childbirth.

It is one of the leading causes of maternal death worldwide.

Risk factors include:

- Prolonged labour
- Multiple pregnancy
- Large baby
- Placental problems
- Previous postpartum hemorrhage

Symptoms include:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding
- Weakness
- Dizziness
- Fast heartbeat
- Low blood pressure
- Loss of consciousness

PPH requires immediate emergency treatment.

---

# Obstructed Labour

Obstructed labour occurs when the baby cannot pass through the birth canal despite strong contractions.

Possible causes include:

- Baby too large
- Abnormal fetal position
- Small maternal pelvis
- Multiple pregnancy

Without treatment, obstructed labour can threaten the lives of both mother and baby.

Emergency Caesarean section may be required.

---

# Caesarean Section

A Caesarean section (C-section) is a surgical operation used to deliver a baby when vaginal birth is not safe.

Common indications include:

- Fetal distress
- Placenta previa
- Breech presentation
- Failure of labour to progress
- Previous uterine surgery
- Multiple pregnancy in selected cases

Most women recover well with appropriate postoperative care.

---

Every delivery should ideally be supervised by trained healthcare professionals.

Benefits include:

- Safe delivery
- Early recognition of complications
- Emergency treatment when required
- Safe newborn care
- Immediate breastfeeding support
- Reduced maternal mortality

# Infection Prevention During Childbirth

Healthcare workers reduce infection by:

- Washing hands properly
- Using sterile equipment
- Wearing protective gloves
- Maintaining a clean delivery environment
- Proper disposal of medical waste
- Administering antibiotics only when indicated

Good infection prevention protects both mother and newborn.

---

# Postnatal Care

The postnatal period begins immediately after childbirth and continues for approximately six weeks. This is an important period for monitoring the health of both the mother and the newborn.

Postnatal care aims to:

- Detect complications early
- Promote recovery after childbirth
- Support breastfeeding
- Monitor emotional well-being
- Provide family planning counselling
- Promote healthy nutrition
- Support newborn care

Women should attend all scheduled postnatal clinic visits.

---

# Recovery After Childbirth

Most women gradually recover over several weeks.

Healthcare providers encourage mothers to:

- Rest adequately
- Eat nutritious meals
- Drink enough clean water
- Continue prescribed medications
- Practice good personal hygiene
- Keep any surgical wounds clean
- Attend follow-up appointments

Recovery time varies between individuals.

---

# Breastfeeding

Breastfeeding provides the ideal nutrition for most babies.

Exclusive breastfeeding is recommended for the first six months of life unless medically contraindicated.

## Benefits for the Baby

- Complete nutrition
- Strong immune protection
- Reduced risk of diarrhea
- Reduced risk of pneumonia
- Healthy brain development
- Lower risk of allergies
- Better growth

## Benefits for the Mother

- Faster uterine contraction
- Reduced postpartum bleeding
- Improved mother–baby bonding
- Lower risk of breast cancer
- Lower risk of ovarian cancer
- Helps return to pre-pregnancy weight

Mothers experiencing breastfeeding difficulties should seek support from healthcare providers.

---

# Family Planning

Family planning helps women and families choose the timing and spacing of pregnancies.

Benefits include:

- Reduced maternal deaths
- Healthier pregnancies
- Improved child health
- Better financial planning
- Reduced pregnancy complications

Common methods include:

- Male condoms
- Female condoms
- Oral contraceptive pills
- Injectable contraceptives
- Implants
- Intrauterine devices (IUDs)
- Lactational Amenorrhea Method (LAM) for eligible breastfeeding mothers

Healthcare providers can help women choose the most suitable option.

---

# Maternal Mental Health

Emotional health is an important part of maternal care.

Many women experience emotional changes after childbirth due to hormonal changes, sleep deprivation, and adjustment to caring for a newborn.

Family support is extremely important.

---

# Baby Blues

Baby blues are common during the first few days after delivery.

Symptoms may include:

- Tearfulness
- Mood swings
- Anxiety
- Irritability
- Difficulty sleeping

These symptoms usually improve within two weeks.

---

# Postpartum Depression

Postpartum depression is more severe than baby blues and requires medical attention.

Symptoms may include:

- Persistent sadness
- Loss of interest in activities
- Hopelessness
- Poor appetite
- Difficulty sleeping
- Excessive fatigue
- Difficulty bonding with the baby
- Feelings of worthlessness
- Thoughts of self-harm

Women experiencing these symptoms should seek professional medical care immediately.

---

# Personal Hygiene After Delivery

Good hygiene reduces infection.

Recommendations include:

- Wash hands regularly.
- Bathe daily.
- Change sanitary pads frequently.
- Keep wounds clean and dry.
- Wear clean clothing.
- Maintain clean bedding.

Seek medical care if wounds become painful, swollen, or produce foul-smelling discharge.

---

# Nutrition After Delivery

Mothers require adequate nutrition during recovery.

Recommended foods include:

- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Whole grains
- Beans
- Fish
- Eggs
- Lean meat
- Milk
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Breastfeeding mothers often require additional calories and fluids.

---

# Maternal Danger Signs After Delivery

Seek immediate medical attention if a mother develops:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding
- Severe abdominal pain
- High fever
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe headache
- Convulsions
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Severe breast pain with fever
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe depression or thoughts of self-harm

Early treatment can save lives.

---

# Preventing Maternal Deaths

Many maternal deaths are preventable.

Important preventive measures include:

- Early antenatal care
- Skilled birth attendance
- Emergency obstetric care
- Adequate nutrition
- Treatment of infections
- Blood pressure monitoring
- Safe delivery practices
- Timely referral of high-risk pregnancies
- Postnatal follow-up
- Family planning

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the nearest hospital immediately if a pregnant or recently delivered woman develops:

- Heavy bleeding
- Difficulty breathing
- Convulsions
- Severe headache
- Blurred vision
- Severe chest pain
- Persistent high fever
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe abdominal pain
- Signs of stroke
- Severe swelling of the face or hands

These symptoms require urgent medical evaluation.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Why is antenatal care important?

Antenatal care helps detect complications early, monitor the baby's growth, provide vaccinations, and prepare mothers for safe delivery.

---

## How many antenatal visits are recommended?

Women should follow the schedule recommended by their healthcare provider. Early and regular visits throughout pregnancy are encouraged.

---

## Can a woman breastfeed after a Caesarean section?

Yes.

Most mothers can successfully breastfeed after a Caesarean section with appropriate support.

---

## How long does postpartum recovery take?

Most women recover within six weeks, although complete recovery may take longer depending on the type of delivery and individual health.

---

## When should family planning begin?

Family planning should be discussed during antenatal care or soon after delivery to help plan future pregnancies safely.
