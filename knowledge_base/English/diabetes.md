# Diabetes Mellitus

## Overview

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic medical condition in which the body is unable to properly regulate blood glucose (blood sugar). This occurs either because the body does not produce enough insulin or because it cannot use insulin effectively.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps glucose move from the bloodstream into the body's cells to provide energy.

Without proper treatment, high blood sugar can damage the heart, kidneys, eyes, nerves, and blood vessels. However, with healthy lifestyle choices, regular monitoring, and appropriate medical treatment, many people with diabetes live long and healthy lives.

---

# Types of Diabetes

## Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes occurs when the body's immune system attacks the insulin-producing cells of the pancreas.

Characteristics include:

- Usually develops during childhood or early adulthood
- The body produces little or no insulin
- Requires lifelong insulin treatment
- Cannot currently be prevented

---

## Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes is the most common form.

Characteristics include:

- The body becomes resistant to insulin.
- The pancreas cannot produce enough insulin to meet the body's needs.
- Often develops gradually.
- Common in adults but increasingly seen in younger people.

Many cases can be prevented or delayed through healthy lifestyle habits.

---

## Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes develops during pregnancy.

It usually resolves after delivery but increases the mother's future risk of developing Type 2 diabetes.

Proper antenatal care is important to protect both mother and baby.

---

# Causes

The causes depend on the type of diabetes.

Possible causes include:

- Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing cells (Type 1)
- Insulin resistance (Type 2)
- Genetic factors
- Family history
- Overweight or obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Hormonal changes during pregnancy

---

# Risk Factors

Risk factors include:

- Family history of diabetes
- Overweight or obesity
- Lack of physical activity
- Unhealthy diet
- High blood pressure
- High cholesterol
- Previous gestational diabetes
- Increasing age
- Certain ethnic backgrounds
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

---

# Signs and Symptoms

Common symptoms include:

- Frequent urination
- Increased thirst
- Increased hunger
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Slow healing of wounds
- Frequent infections
- Dry mouth
- Tingling or numbness in the hands or feet

Some people with Type 2 diabetes may have no symptoms for many years.

---

# Severe Symptoms

Seek immediate medical care if the patient develops:

- Difficulty breathing
- Confusion
- Severe weakness
- Persistent vomiting
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe dehydration
- Seizures
- Severe abdominal pain
- Fruity-smelling breath
- Inability to eat or drink

These symptoms may indicate a diabetic emergency.

---

# Possible Complications

Poorly controlled diabetes can lead to:

- Heart disease
- Stroke
- Kidney disease
- Kidney failure
- Vision loss
- Blindness
- Nerve damage (neuropathy)
- Foot ulcers
- Foot infections
- Amputation
- Sexual dysfunction
- Gum disease

Good blood sugar control greatly reduces these risks.

---

# Diagnosis

Healthcare professionals may diagnose diabetes using:

- Fasting blood glucose test
- Random blood glucose test
- HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin)
- Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
- Urine glucose testing (supportive)
- Urine ketone testing (when indicated)

Diagnosis should always be confirmed by a qualified healthcare professional.

---

# Treatment

Treatment depends on the type of diabetes.

Management may include:

- Healthy eating
- Regular physical activity
- Weight management
- Blood sugar monitoring
- Oral diabetes medicines
- Insulin therapy
- Regular medical follow-up

Patients should take medications exactly as prescribed.

Never stop diabetes medication without consulting a healthcare professional.

---

# Healthy Lifestyle

Lifestyle plays an important role in diabetes management.

Recommendations include:

- Exercise regularly
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Eat balanced meals
- Limit sugary drinks
- Reduce processed foods
- Stop smoking
- Limit alcohol
- Get adequate sleep
- Manage stress

Healthy habits improve blood sugar control and reduce complications.

---

# Nutrition

A healthy diet may include:

- Vegetables
- Fruits (in appropriate portions)
- Whole grains
- Beans
- Fish
- Skinless poultry
- Lean meat
- Low-fat dairy products
- Nuts
- Plenty of clean drinking water

Limit:

- Sugary drinks
- Sweets
- Refined carbohydrates
- Deep-fried foods
- Highly processed foods

Meal planning should be individualized where possible.

---

# Home Care

People living with diabetes should:

- Monitor blood sugar as advised
- Take medicines regularly
- Eat healthy meals
- Stay physically active
- Inspect their feet daily
- Keep wounds clean
- Attend regular medical appointments
- Monitor blood pressure and cholesterol
- Maintain a healthy body weight

Good self-care helps prevent complications.

---

# Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)

Hypoglycemia may occur if blood sugar becomes too low.

Possible symptoms include:

- Sweating
- Trembling
- Hunger
- Dizziness
- Fast heartbeat
- Headache
- Irritability
- Confusion

If the person is awake and able to swallow, give a fast-acting source of sugar (such as glucose tablets or a sugary drink) and seek medical advice if symptoms do not improve.

If the person is unconscious or unable to swallow, seek emergency medical care immediately.

---

# Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar)

High blood sugar may cause:

- Extreme thirst
- Frequent urination
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Dry mouth
- Nausea

Persistent high blood sugar requires medical evaluation and treatment adjustment.

---

# Prevention

Type 1 diabetes cannot currently be prevented.

The risk of Type 2 diabetes may be reduced by:

- Maintaining a healthy weight
- Regular physical activity
- Eating a balanced diet
- Limiting sugary foods and drinks
- Avoiding tobacco
- Limiting alcohol
- Regular health screening
- Managing blood pressure and cholesterol

---

# When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the nearest hospital immediately if the patient develops:

- Loss of consciousness
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe confusion
- Persistent vomiting
- Severe dehydration
- Seizures
- Severe chest pain
- Signs of stroke
- Very low blood sugar that does not improve
- Very high blood sugar with severe symptoms

Prompt treatment can be life-saving.

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Can diabetes be cured?

There is currently no cure for most forms of diabetes, but it can be effectively managed with healthy lifestyle choices and appropriate treatment.

---

## Can diabetes be prevented?

Type 1 diabetes cannot currently be prevented.

Many cases of Type 2 diabetes can be prevented or delayed through healthy eating, regular exercise, and maintaining a healthy weight.

---

## Can people with diabetes eat fruit?

Yes.

Most people with diabetes can eat fruit in appropriate portions as part of a balanced diet.

---

## Why is foot care important?

Diabetes can reduce sensation and slow wound healing. Daily foot inspection helps prevent serious infections and amputations.

---

## Should I stop my medicine when I feel well?

No.

Blood sugar may remain controlled because the treatment is working. Always follow your healthcare provider's advice.

---

# Key Points

- Diabetes is a chronic condition affecting blood sugar control.
- Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes have different causes.
- Healthy eating, exercise, and medication help control diabetes.
- Regular monitoring reduces complications.
- Good foot care is essential.
- Early diagnosis and treatment improve long-term health.
- Seek emergency care for severe symptoms such as unconsciousness, severe confusion, or difficulty breathing.
